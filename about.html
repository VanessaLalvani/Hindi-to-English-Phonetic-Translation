Almost

On language, memory, and the space between knowing and not knowing.

Thesis Statement

The Work

This work explores the fragile space between language, memory, and identity through the lens of partial understanding.

As an Indian who does not speak Hindi, I navigate a world where recognition comes easily but comprehension remains out of reach. My design practice uses phonetics, translation, and visual play to make this in-between state tangible, revealing what it feels like to decipher a heritage language through sound rather than fluency.

This project transforms the process of learning Hindi phonetics into an emotional, interactive experience. By pairing Devanagari letters with English sound approximations, audio cues, and playful book-based games, it recreates the slow, uncertain, but intimate process of almost understanding a language you were never taught but always lived around.

"Each sound, shape, and syllable becomes a fragment of a language I know emotionally before I know intellectually."
The Problem

A Liminal
Existence

As a second-generation Indian who doesn't speak Hindi, I exist in a liminal space.

I recognize the rhythm, tone, and emotion of my mother tongue, but its meaning remains elusive. I see the visuals of my culture, yet feel a persistent sense of not fully belonging. The generational loss of language, or language attrition, creates a void: a barrier to true connection with my family, often met with laughter that, I now realize, is a defense mechanism rather than mockery.

This laughter is layered with shame, defensiveness, and loss.

My design is not meant to explain this wound. It is meant to make the user feel the exact sensation of being on the cusp of meaning.

The System

How It
Works

Only 39% of Hindi consonants have perfect English matches. The rest live in gaps.

This learning system is built on honesty. Rather than pretending every Hindi sound maps cleanly to English, each letter is tagged with a translation strategy, a badge that names the type of gap between the two languages.

DIRECT MATCH

The sound exists naturally in English. No approximation needed.

APPROXIMATION

English has a close equivalent, but tongue position or breath differs subtly.

LIMINAL

The sound sits in a gap English doesn't acknowledge: aspiration, retroflexion, or both.

POSITIONAL

The sound exists in English but only within words, never at the start.

COMPOUND

Two sounds fused into one letter. A conjunct, not a new invention.

"The goal is not fluency. It is to translate the emotional reality of almost understanding."
Design Intention

Reframing
Disconn-
ection

The goal isn't to fix the disconnection. It's to reframe it.

Through a website that pairs Devanagari characters with English-based sounds and audio cues, and a companion book filled with phonetic games, the work turns the act of learning into a metaphor for reconnection.

By tracing sounds, listening closely, and assembling meaning piece by piece, the audience mirrors my own slow reconstruction of a language that was inherited but never fully grasped. The recognition without mastery. The closeness mixed with distance. The feeling of being connected and disconnected at the same time.

Research Question

How can graphic design translate the visceral, emotional experience of almost understanding a heritage language to an audience who shares a similar generational gap?

This is not a language course.
It is a record of something half-remembered,
half-felt, and never quite lost.
